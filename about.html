Early Life

Withdrawal From Family

Read more

When Nathaniel's father becomes consumed by his late-night Chemistry work with Coppelius, he completely withdraws from his family, so much so that Nathaniel asks his mother, "Eh, mamma, who is this naughty Sandman, who always drives us away from papa? What does he look like? (Hoffman 87)" The father's participation in scientific experiments "drives" him away from his family, and instead of gathering with him, the children are sent away and learn to associate their father's work with danger.

Prioritization of Science over Family

Read more

A year after Coppelius stopped coming to the house, Nathaniel recalls that his "father was very cheerful." However, "when as the clock struck nine, we heard the house-door creak… ‘That is Coppelius,’ said my mother, turning pale… The tears started from my mother’s eyes. ‘But father—father!’ she cried, ‘must it be so?’ ‘He comes to me… go with the children—go—go to bed.’ (Hoffman 93)” Although his wife was clearly unhappy, and his children had to leave and hide from his partner, Nathaniel's father chose to further his research. The father's scientific pursuits showing how he prioritized Coppelius and scientific advancements over comfort, safety, and the overall relationship with his family.

What She Represents

Read more

Olympia represents and idealized, yet unreal partner that sets standards so high that it simply is not achievable. Nathaniel wants to attain this perfection, when in reality he should be focusing on connection.

Obsession with Olympia

Read more

Olympia is docile, agreeable, and beautiful, but she is a robot and has no true feelings for herself. Nathaniel becomes obsessed with her and neglects all other aspects of his life. "Clara's image had been wiped clean from his mind" (Hoffmann 112) and "his mother, Lothario, all had vanished from memory" (Hoffmann 117).

Rejection of his other Relationships

Read more

Nathaniel prefers the “predictability” and “passiveness” of Olympia over the real, flawed human relationships (like with Clara). Nathaniel became so obsessed with Olympia that he neglected to foster his true relationships, abandoning his letter to Clara and shutting off his family from memory. Through this example, Hoffmann critiques the way technology can distance us from authentic social bonds.
